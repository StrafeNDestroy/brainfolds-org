    Chapter 9: Water Movement in Soil

    Why this matters: Understanding how water moves through your specific soil — how fast it drains, how far it wicks, where it collects — is the foundation of irrigation design, drainage solutions, water harvesting, and drought management. This chapter bridges soil science directly into practical land and water management.

    Part B — Short Answer

    1. What is the difference between infiltration and percolation?

    Model answer:
    Infiltration: entry of water at the soil surface (rate limited by surface condition, texture, structure). Percolation: downward movement through soil profile after entering (rate limited by texture, structure, restrictive layers).

    2. What is field capacity and why is it important?

    Model answer:
    Field capacity: the moisture level after free drainage has ceased — soil holds the maximum water against gravity. Important because it represents the "full tank" level for plant water availability calculations.

    3. What is plant available water?

    Model answer:
    Plant available water: moisture plants can actually extract — between field capacity (upper limit) and permanent wilting point (lower limit). Loam holds ~1.5-2 inches per foot; clay ~2-2.5 inches; sand ~0.75-1 inch.

    4. How does soil texture affect water movement?

    Model answer:
    Sand: fast infiltration, fast percolation, low retention. Clay: slow infiltration, slow percolation, high retention. Loam: intermediate on all. Structure also matters: well-aggregated clay can drain faster than compacted loam.

    5. What is capillary rise and how deep can it move water?

    Model answer:
    Capillary rise: soil water moves upward through small pores by surface tension. Distance: sand ~20cm, silt ~100cm, clay ~300cm but very slowly. Important for plant access to water table in some conditions.

    6. What is a perched water table and what causes it?

    Model answer:
    Perched water table forms above an impermeable layer (hardpan, clay lens) that blocks downward percolation. Saturates the zone above it, causing waterlogging even in otherwise well-drained soils.

    7. How does mulch affect soil water content?

    Model answer:
    Mulch reduces surface evaporation by 50-70%, moderates soil temperature, prevents surface crusting. 2-4 inches of organic mulch is one of the most effective water conservation practices.

    8. What is evapotranspiration and how do you calculate irrigation needs?

    Model answer:
    ET = evaporation from soil surface + transpiration from plants. For irrigation: irrigate to replace ET since last irrigation. Texas ET maps provide reference values. Crop coefficients adjust for specific crops.

    9. Describe how deep to irrigate and why.

    Model answer:
    Irrigate to the depth of the active root zone — typically 12-18 inches for vegetables, 24-36+ for fruit trees. Shallow irrigation promotes shallow roots, making plants vulnerable to drought.

    10. How do macropores and micropores differ in their water-holding functions?

    Model answer:
    Macropores (>0.5mm): conduct water during infiltration, drain quickly, then fill with air needed by roots. Micropores (0.05-0.5mm): hold water at field capacity, available to plants. Ultramicropores (<0.05mm): hold water so tightly plants cannot extract it.

    Part D — Practical Exercises

    Complete in the field. Check each off as you go.

    1. Infiltration Rate Test: Pour 1 cup of water into a cylinder pressed into the soil. Time absorption. Repeat in 3 locations. Compare: native grassland, vegetable garden, and bare compacted area.

    2. Soil Moisture at Different Depths: Dig down 6", 12", 18", and 24" after a significant rain (1"+). Feel the soil at each depth. How deep did moisture penetrate? How long after rain does it persist at each depth?

    3. Mulch vs Bare Soil Comparison: Mulch a 4×4 foot bed 3" deep. Leave an identical bed bare. After 2 weeks without rain, dig to 6" in each. Which retains moisture better?

    4. ET Estimation: Look up your county's reference evapotranspiration (ET₀) on the Texas ET Network (texaset.tamu.edu). Calculate how much water your vegetable garden needs per week.

    5. Percolation Test (for septic/drainage assessment): Dig a 12"×12"×12" hole. Fill with water to presaturate soil. After 24 hours, fill again. Record the time for water to drop from 6" to 5" (1 inch drop). Calculate percolation rate in min/inch.
