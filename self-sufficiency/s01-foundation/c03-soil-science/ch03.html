    Chapter 3: Soil Structure — Aggregates, Pores, and Tilth

    Why this matters: Two soils with identical texture can perform completely differently depending on their structure. Structure is what you build over years of good management — it determines drainage, aeration, root penetration, and workability. "Good tilth" is what every farmer and gardener is ultimately working toward.

    Part B — Short Answer

    1. What is a soil aggregate and what holds aggregates together?

    Model answer:
    Aggregates are clusters of soil particles. Held by: fungal hyphae (physically enmesh), bacterial biofilms, glomalin (protein), humus, root exudates, earthworm casts, and electrostatic attraction on clay.

    2. Describe five types of soil structure and where each is typically found.

    Model answer:
    Granular: small rounded peds, ideal for topsoil/crops. Platy: flat horizontal layers, indicates compaction or tillage pan. Blocky: cube-like, common in B horizon. Prismatic: tall vertical columns, subsoil clays. Crumb: very small granular, best for plant growth.

    3. What is the slake test and what does it measure?

    Model answer:
    Dry aggregate dropped into water. Stable aggregates resist disintegration. Unstable ones immediately disperse. Measures organic matter content, biological activity, and tillage history.

    4. How does tillage affect soil structure over time?

    Model answer:
    Tillage initially aerates but breaks aggregates, destroys fungal networks, exposes organic matter to oxidation, kills earthworms. Long-term: organic matter declines, structure degrades, requiring more tillage.

    5. What is glomalin and why is it important for soil structure?

    Model answer:
    Glomalin is a glycoprotein secreted by arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi that coats hyphae and soil particles. It is a major component of soil organic matter that binds aggregates together. Accounts for up to 27% of soil carbon.

    6. How do earthworms contribute to soil structure?

    Model answer:
    Earthworms consume organic matter and mineral particles, excrete as casts — highly stable aggregates with 5x the nutrients. Their burrows create macropores for water and air movement.

    7. What farming practices best preserve or rebuild soil structure?

    Model answer:
    No-till farming, cover crops, diverse rotations, adding compost/manure, avoiding heavy machinery on wet soil, maintaining living roots year-round.

    8. Why is soil structure important for roots?

    Model answer:
    Good structure: large connected pores allow oxygen, water, and root penetration. Poor structure: tight dense matrix restricts all three, reducing root depth and function.

    9. How does compaction affect soil structure?

    Model answer:
    Compaction: destroys aggregates, eliminates macropores, reduces oxygen, inhibits biology, limits root growth, causes runoff instead of infiltration.

    10. Describe the relationship between soil structure and water movement.

    Model answer:
    Well-structured soil: large pores (macropores) allow rapid infiltration then distribution through mesopores to roots. Poor structure: either fast runoff (no aggregates, crusted) or waterlogged (compacted clay).

    Part D — Practical Exercises

    Complete in the field. Check each off as you go.

    1. Slake Test: Collect dry soil aggregates from 3 locations (garden bed, pasture, bare compacted area). Drop into water. Observe which disintegrate quickly vs hold together. Rate structural stability 1-5.

    2. Tillage Comparison: Compare soil from a regularly tilled garden to an untilled no-disturbed area. Feel the difference. Dig to 12 inches and observe: which has more earthworms? Better aggregation?

    3. Cover Crop Experiment: In fall, plant winter rye or crimson clover in a section of bare soil. Terminate in spring. Compare soil structure to untreated area after one season.

    4. Earthworm Count: Count earthworms in a 12"×12"×12" block of soil from 5 different areas of your property. Which area has the most? Why? What does earthworm count indicate?

    5. Root Penetration Observation: Dig up a plant root system from a well-structured soil and from a compacted area. Note the difference in root branching, depth, and overall health.
