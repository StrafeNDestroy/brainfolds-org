    Chapter 8: Mycorrhizal Fungi — The Underground Network

    Why this matters: Mycorrhizal fungi are arguably the most important organisms in soil for plant establishment and long-term health. Understanding them explains why transplanted plants fail, why native plants thrive without fertilizer, and why disturbing soil biology can set a restoration project back by years.

    Part B — Short Answer

    1. What is the difference between ectomycorrhizae and arbuscular (AM) mycorrhizae?

    Model answer:
    Ectomycorrhizae: surround root tips in a dense sheath, hyphae penetrate between cells (Hartig net) but not inside cells. Form with oaks, pines, beeches. Arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM): hyphae penetrate through cell wall into the cell membrane, forming arbuscules (branched feeding structures). Form with 80%+ of plant species including most crops.

    2. What is the nutrient exchange between a plant and its mycorrhizal fungi?

    Model answer:
    Plant provides: photosynthate (sugars, up to 20-30% of total plant carbon production). Fungi provide: phosphorus, nitrogen, water, zinc, copper, and other minerals from distant soil.

    3. Why does high phosphorus fertilization reduce mycorrhizal colonization?

    Model answer:
    When P is abundant, the plant has no reason to trade carbon for it. The plant reduces carbon allocation to fungi, reducing colonization. High P fertilization thus disconnects plants from the fungal network.

    4. What is glomalin and why is it important?

    Model answer:
    Glomalin: a stable glycoprotein secreted by AM fungi that coats hyphae and soil particles. Binds aggregates together, major component of stable soil organic matter, contributes to long-term carbon storage.

    5. Describe the "wood wide web" — what it is and what it does.

    Model answer:
    The wood wide web: the network of mycorrhizal hyphae connecting trees and other plants. Allows: carbon transfer from mother trees to seedlings, nutrient sharing between species, chemical signaling about pests.

    6. Which agricultural practices damage mycorrhizal networks?

    Model answer:
    Tillage (destroys hyphae), fumigation (kills fungi), high P fertilizer (reduces need), fungicides, bare soil (breaks network without living roots), monocultures (reduce fungal diversity).

    7. Why are brassicas poor companion plants for mycorrhizal plant species?

    Model answer:
    Brassicas (Brassicaceae) do not form mycorrhizal associations and may produce antifungal compounds (glucosinolates). Planting them near mycorrhizal crops can reduce colonization in neighboring plants.

    8. How do mycorrhizae help plants survive drought?

    Model answer:
    Mycorrhizal hyphae access pores too small for roots, extending the absorbing surface 100-1000x. In drought, when soil dries and capillary water movement stops, hyphae can still access water in micropores.

    9. What are mycorrhizal inoculants and when are they useful?

    Model answer:
    Mycorrhizal inoculants: commercial spore mixes. Useful when: transplanting into sterile soil, degraded soils lacking native fungi, crops with high colonization potential, establishing native plants. Less useful in naturally inoculated field soils.

    10. How do mycorrhizal networks benefit a forest ecosystem?

    Model answer:
    Mycorrhizal networks: allow carbon from established trees to support seedlings in shade, warn neighboring trees of pest attack (chemical signals), link species across gaps, and maintain productivity when one species declines.

    Part D — Practical Exercises

    Complete in the field. Check each off as you go.

    1. Mycorrhizal Observation: Carefully dig up roots of an established oak or native grass. Look for ectomycorrhizal sheaths (visible white/tan coating on root tips) or AM colonization (requires clearing/staining). Compare to roots of a recently transplanted plant.

    2. Phosphorus Trade-off Test: Grow three identical tomato seedlings: (1) inoculated with mycorrhizae + no P fertilizer, (2) no inoculant + standard P fertilizer, (3) inoculated + standard P fertilizer. Measure growth and root development after 8 weeks.

    3. Brassica vs Tomato Spacing: Plant tomatoes next to brassicas (cabbage, broccoli) and separately from brassicas. After 6 weeks, compare tomato growth. Brassicas may reduce mycorrhizal colonization of neighboring plants.

    4. Glomalin Test: Collect soil from under an established native oak woodland and from a repeatedly tilled garden. Do a slake test on both. The woodland soil should have far more stable aggregates due to glomalin.

    5. Network Transfer: In a forest, find two trees connected at the base by visible fungal structures. Research which tree species in Texas are known to share mycorrhizal networks. Note any nurse-seedling relationships you can observe.
