    Chapter 4: Soil pH — What It Is and Why It Matters

    Why this matters: pH controls what nutrients are available to your plants regardless of how much fertilizer you add. A plant with yellowing leaves in a nutrient-rich soil is often not nutrient-deficient — it just can't access the nutrients because pH is wrong. Understanding pH is the difference between treating symptoms and solving the actual problem.

    Part B — Short Answer

    1. What is soil pH and why does it matter for plant nutrition?

    Model answer:
    Soil pH measures hydrogen ion concentration. It controls nutrient availability — most nutrients have a pH-specific availability window. Wrong pH locks up nutrients regardless of their presence in soil.

    2. What is the ideal pH range for most vegetable crops?

    Model answer:
    pH 6.0-6.8 for most vegetables. Some exceptions: potatoes prefer slightly acidic 5.5-6.0; brassicas tolerate 6.0-7.5; blueberries require 4.5-5.5.

    3. Why are Texas soils often alkaline?

    Model answer:
    Texas parent material is largely limestone (calcium carbonate, CaCO3). Weathering releases calcium and carbonate ions. Low rainfall doesn\'t leach these out. Result: pH 7.5-8.5 across much of Central and West Texas.

    4. What is iron chlorosis and how does pH cause it?

    Model answer:
    At pH above 7.5, iron forms insoluble compounds the plant cannot absorb. Plant shows interveinal chlorosis on young leaves — iron is mobile in plant, chlorosis appears on new growth.

    5. How do you lower soil pH?

    Model answer:
    Lower pH: elemental sulfur (oxidized by bacteria to sulfuric acid), acidifying fertilizers (ammonium sulfate), acidic mulches (pine bark, peat). Note: caliche soils are very difficult to acidify permanently.

    6. How do you raise soil pH?

    Model answer:
    Raise pH: add agricultural lime (calcium carbonate), dolomite (magnesium+calcium carbonate), or wood ash. Rate depends on soil buffer capacity — sandy soils need less lime than clays.

    7. What is caliche and what problems does it create?

    Model answer:
    Caliche: hardened calcium carbonate layer in subsoil, common in Texas. Creates: drainage impediment (perched water table), root barrier, high pH above it, nutrient lockup.

    8. Which nutrients become less available at high pH?

    Model answer:
    Iron, manganese, zinc, copper, and boron become less available above pH 7.0-7.5. Phosphorus has a double peak — ties up at both high and low pH, most available around 6.5.

    9. How do you test soil pH at home?

    Model answer:
    Vinegar test (fizz = alkaline), litmus paper, commercial pH test kit with indicator solution, digital pH meter with electrode. All available at garden centers.

    10. Name three acid-loving plants and describe how you would grow them in alkaline Texas soil.

    Model answer:
    Southern Highbush blueberries (e.g., Misty, O\'Neal) tolerate slightly higher pH. Amend raised beds with peat, pine bark, sulfur to pH 4.5-5.5. Use acidifying fertilizers. Monitor pH seasonally.

    Part D — Practical Exercises

    Complete in the field. Check each off as you go.

    1. pH Testing: Purchase a pH test kit or strips. Test soil from 5 different spots on your property. Test: under live oak, in the vegetable garden, in a grass area, near caliche, and from a compost pile. Compare results.

    2. Iron Chlorosis Identification: Walk your property and look for plants with yellow leaves and green veins (especially on young growth). This is iron chlorosis. Which plant types show it most?

    3. Sulfur Application Test: Apply elemental sulfur at the recommended rate to a 4×4 ft plot. Test pH monthly for 6 months. How quickly does it change?
